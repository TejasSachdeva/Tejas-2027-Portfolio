Battery Cooling Manifold for Solar Racing Vehicle

CFD Design Cross-Functionality Manufacturing

Project Overview

This project focused on designing and optimizing a thermal intake and exhaust manifold for the solar vehicle’s battery system. The objective was to maintain safe operating temperatures under endurance race conditions, where battery packs experience fluctuating thermal loads. The system needed to balance airflow distribution, pressure drop, and heat dissipation to improve pack longevity and ensure reliable vehicle performance throughout extended runs.

Design and CAD-CFD Integration

The cooling system consisted of a forward-facing intake duct paired with an exhaust plenum that directed airflow across the battery pack. CAD models were iteratively refined in SolidWorks and exported into ANSYS Fluent for analysis. Parametric design studies varied duct geometry, bend radii, and flow splitters to minimize separation zones and balance airflow across modules. By integrating CAD and CFD workflows, we could quickly iterate designs while tracking the effect of geometry changes on both flow uniformity and thermal gradients. Trade-offs centered around maximizing convective cooling efficiency without exceeding the available fan capacity.

Simulation and Validation

Steady-state and transient CFD analyses quantified temperature distribution, heat flux, and pressure drop across the system. Initial simulations revealed stagnation regions that led to cell-level hot spots. By reshaping duct transitions and optimizing outlet geometry, we reduced localized peak temperatures by over 20% and achieved more uniform cooling across the pack. Pressure drop was maintained below ~150 Pa, ensuring compatibility with the team’s selected fans. Final results showed that the design effectively prevented thermal runaway risks, keeping cells within the safe operating envelope during simulated endurance cycles.

Manufacturing and Implementation

The final intake and exhaust manifold were 3D printed using PETG, chosen for its balance of strength, thermal resistance, and ease of fabrication. Smooth internal channels reduced turbulence, while mounting bosses and alignment features ensured repeatable assembly with the battery housing. Considerations were also made for future instrumentation, including thermocouple and flow sensor integration, to validate CFD predictions with on-track data. The PETG prototype provided a durable, lightweight solution that could be rapidly iterated for subsequent design cycles.

Technical Specifications

Dimensions and Layout
  • Intake length: 13 in; diffuser expansion ratio: 3.2:1
  • Geometry optimized for battery enclosure integration
Material
  • 3D-printed PETG throughout (prototype and final)
  • Heat-resistant to ~80 °C, lightweight (≈ 350 g)
Flow Path & Geometry
  • Curved diffuser with 15° half-angle for pressure recovery
  • Internal guide vanes to reduce velocity gradients
Cooling Performance
  • CFD-validated mass flow ≈ 0.065 kg/s at 20 m/s vehicle speed
  • Predicted cell temperature drop: ~4 °C under peak load
Mounting and Integration
  • Quick-release fastener system for serviceability
  • Interfaces directly with liquid-cooled battery manifold
